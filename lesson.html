1. What is a Touchscreen?

The touchscreen, also referred to as touch screen, is a technology that allows the user to provide input (as a touch panel) and also to obtain output (as a display) in the process of interaction with a computational device.

The main role of a touchscreen is to facilitate the user to interact directly with the information displayed on the screen, through tactile means.

In today's world, touchscreens are the predominant types of input methods. From the most popular devices that are incorporating a touchscreen, we can enumerate:

  • Smart-phones
  • Tablets
  • Laptops
  • PC monitors
  • E-books
  • Printers
  • Car's multimedia systems
  • POS machines (Fig.1)
  • Ticket machines
  • Smart-watches
  • Etc…

2. History of Touchscreen Technology

Although there are various ideas and patents recorded as the first touchscreen developed, such as the device developed by AT&T Corporation in 1962, under the name of 'Electrographic transmitter', generally, the invention of touchscreen is attributed in 1965 to Eric Johnson, of the Royal Radar Establishment, in England.

In early 1970s, two engineers from CERN (European Organization for Nuclear Research), announced the development of a transparent touchscreen which has been practically implemented by different industries in 1973.

The first multi-touch display has been invented by an Input Research Group, including Bill Buxton at the University of Toronto in 1982.

3. Touchscreen Technology

This lesson is based on the YouTube video 'How Do Touchscreens Work?', presented by Branch Education and we will focus generally on how the capacitive touchscreens are working in smartphones devices. The complete video can be found at the end of the lesson, with video time-marks throughout the article for a more comprehensive presentation.

The most common types of screens are made from glass or plastic. In Fig.2 can be seen a table that is generally presenting the pros and cons between the two. This presentation will focus on the glass screens.

There are 3 elements that form a touchscreen: toughened glass, capacitive touchscreen and OLED (Organic Light Emitting Diode) display. These elements are assembled one on top of the other 0:50.

The top layer is made of chemically strengthened glass which gives resistance to impact 2:15. The strengthened glass is usually about 5 times stronger than the normal glass. In smartphone industry, before 2007 when the first iPhone was released, the general standard for smartphone screens was plastic which was easily scratchable, now replaced with glass for a more robust aspect.

The projected capacitive touchscreen is found underneath the toughened glass and is responsible for the detection and interpretation of the tactile presence. This element is composed from two transparent diamond grid patterns printed on polyester with a clear insulator positioned in between those. The diamond grids pattern layers are interacting with each other through a combination of positive and negative electric fields, forming a capacitor 3:15. When a conductive material (tip of the finger, stylus pen, etc) is in the proximity of this capacitor, it disrupts the electric field, changing the amount of positive and negative charges in between the diamond grids. This operation is interpreted by the processor as a touch 4:01. The location of the touch on the screen's surface is identified by scanning the charges and voltage along the grid pattern 4:22.

Below the capacitor we can find the LCD (Liquid Crystal Display) or the OLED (Organic Light Emitting Diode) 5:03. In this lesson we will concentrate on OLED technology, which can generate high resolution images that can be observed and interact with when we are using our smartphones. In 2018 high-end smartphones were reaching 3.3 million pixels. One pixel is constructed from 3 microscopic, individually controlled and dimmable lights of red, blue and green 5:45. The image seen on the screen is a grid composited from millions of such pixels.
